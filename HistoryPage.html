Bahrain has a wonderful and fruitful history, full of interesting and special events that have contributed to the cultural growth of the Arabian Gulf.

ANCIENT BAHRAIN

DILMUN
Dilmun is first mentioned in Sumerian cuneiform clay tablets from the end of the 4th millennium BC, found in the temple of goddess Inanna, in the city of Uruk. Sennacherib, king of Assyria (707-681) BC, attacked the northeast Persian Gulf and captured Bahrain, according to a monument discovered in Bahrain.
PERSIAN EMPIRE
Bahrain played an important role in the Persian Empire from the 6th century BC until the 3rd century BC. There were also two Iranian dynasties controlling Bahrain, the Parthians and the Sassanids. In the 3rd century AD, the Sassanids succeeded the Parthians and held the area until the arrival of Islam four centuries later.
TYLOS
When Nearchus, a Greek admiral serving under Alexander the Great, overthrew Al Hamar's rule, Bahrain was called Tylos, and it was the centre of pearl trading. Alexander planned to settle the eastern shores of the Persian Gulf with Greek colonists, but it is not known whether Bahrain was part of the Seleucid Empire. The archaeological site at Qalat Al Bahrain has been proposed as a Seleucid base.

ISLAM IN BAHRAIN

When Bahrain adopted Islam in 629 (the seventh year of hijra), the Prophet Mohammed ruled Bahrain through Al-Ala'a Al-Hadhrami. Under Umar I, Abu Hurayrah was the governor of Bahrain.

Established in 692, Al Khamis Mosque was one of the first mosques built in Bahrain during the reign of Umar II, the Umayyad caliph. Bahrain was a center of Nestorian Christianity before Islam.

Bahrain's dependence on trade did not change with Islam, and its prosperity continued to be dependent on markets in Mesopotamia. When Baghdad became the caliphate and the center of Islamic civilization in 750, Bahrain greatly benefited from the increase in the city's demand for imported goods, particularly from China and South Asia.

DYNASTIES

QARMATIANS
Approximately 900 years ago, Abu Sa'id al-Hasan al-Jannabi led a rebellion by a messianic Ismaili sect originating in present-day Kufa, Iraq. Al-Jannabi seized Hajr, Bahrain's capital at the time. Qarmatians aimed to build a society that was based on reason and equality. Initiates had equal access to the property within their community. Although Qarmatian society was esoteric, their activities were public and openly propagated.
UYUNID
With the help of the Great Seljuq Empire's armies, Sheikh Abdullah bin Ali Al Uyuni captured the nation from the Qarmatians in Bahrain and Al-Hasa in 1076â€“1077, establishing the Uyunid dynasty and the Uyunid Emirate. From the 11th to the 13th centuries, the Uyunid dynasty ruled Bahrain for 163 years.
JABRID
Zamil ibn Jabir took control of Bahrain in the mid-15th century, establishing the Bedouin Jabrid dynasty. The Jabrids dominated most of eastern Arabia from al-Ahsa, and they practiced the Sunni Maliki rite, which they zealously propagated within their territory.
PORTUGESE
Attracted by the rich trade routes of the Gulf, the Portuguese wanted control of the key Ormus region before moving on to Bahrain. In 1485, Portuguese ships first entered the Gulf, and Duarte Barbosa is said to have been the first Portuguese traveller to explore Bahrain. Hormuz' governmental authority of Bahrain was lost once the Kingdom of Hormuz fell in 1507, when the island passed to the prince of Al-Hasa.

THE BRITISH AND BAHRAIN

The Al Khalifa tribe reclaimed control in Bahrain in 1820 and signed a treaty with the United Kingdom, which was then the main military power in the Persian Gulf. This contract established the Al Khalifa as Bahrain's rulers ("Al-Hakim" in Arabic). The 1861 Perpetual Truce of Peace and Friendship, which was revised in 1892 and 1951, was the first of multiple treaties.

This pact was identical to the ones that the British government had made with the other Persian Gulf princedoms. It said that the ruler could not sell any of his land to anyone other than the United Kingdom, and that he could not form any relationships with foreign governments without British permission. In exchange, the British vowed to defend Bahrain from any maritime incursion and to assist in the event of a land attack. More crucially, the British committed to back Bahrain's Al Khalifa rulers.
